Helping Kids Manage Exam Anxieties

February 22, 2025 Children's Mental Health
Blog Image

At some point in your lives, you will encounter the joys and challenges of dealing with your children's exam-related anxieties. This comprehensive guide addresses exam-related stress in students from SATs through GCSEs.

Creating Supportive Environments

Establish spaces where students feel comfortable expressing concerns without judgment. Regular one-on-one check-ins can help children feel heard and supported during stressful periods.

Effective Study Methods

Breaking revision into manageable segments prevents overwhelm. Employ varied techniques to keep studying engaging:

  • Flashcards for key facts and definitions
  • Past papers for exam practice
  • Study groups for collaborative learning
  • Mind maps for visual learners

Stress Management Techniques

Several approaches can help manage exam anxiety:

Breathing Exercises

Inhale deeply for four seconds, hold the breath for four seconds, and exhale for four seconds. Repeat this cycle several times when feeling anxious.

Visualisation

Encourage children to visualise success - imagine walking into the exam feeling confident and answering questions calmly.

Progressive Muscle Relaxation

Tensing and then releasing different muscle groups can help release physical tension associated with anxiety.

Self-Care Practices

Physical wellbeing directly impacts mental health. Ensure your child maintains:

  • Regular physical activity
  • Balanced nutrition
  • Adequate sleep (8-10 hours for teenagers)

Exam-Day Preparation

Practical preparation reduces anxiety on the day:

  • Arrive early to avoid rushing
  • Bring all necessary materials
  • Read questions carefully
  • Manage time effectively during the exam

Post-Exam Support

After exams, engage children in enjoyable activities and remind them that their worth is not defined solely by exam results. Explore diverse career pathways and celebrate their efforts regardless of outcomes.

Perspective and Resilience

Mental well-being matters more than scores alone. Promote self-compassion and realistic expectations. Help children understand that exams are just one part of their journey, not the whole story.
